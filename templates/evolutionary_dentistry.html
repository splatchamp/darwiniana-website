The Appendix

The first vestigial organ that comes to mind is the appendix, which is found in many mammal species. Rodents, rabbits, and wombats have an appendix-like organ while the taxonomic family Hominidae (genuses Pan, Pongo, Gorilla, & Homo) have appendices more familiar to us. Many other Old World primates, those found in Africa and Asia, also have appendices. The so-called lower primates of the New World (South America) and the prosimians do not have appendices, however (G.B. Scott, 1980). The prevalence of the appendix should pose to us a question: is the appendix an adaptation? Using the Three-part Test of Adaptation (Heather Heying & Bret Weinstein, A Hunter-Gatherer's Guide to the 21st Century),
  1. Is it complex? Yes
  2. Does it have energetic or material costs, which vary between individuals? Yes
  3. Has it persisted over evolutionary time? Yes
But isn't the appendix vestigial in humans? Well, the rate of appendicitis has not remained constant over the decades. If the appendix were vestigial, we would expect rates of appendicitis to stay constant for a longer period of time than is the case. We would also expect humans all over the globe to be experiencing appendicitis at similar rates. Ferris et al. (2017) find that the period during which a country industrializes coincides with a growing rate of appendicitis. The United States (including Alaska but not Canada), Italy, New Zealand, and the Scandinavian countries were already experiencing high rates of appendicitis before 1970, namely >= 150 per 100,000. Since 1970, Canada, Mexico, other European countries, and parts of Africa and South America have joined the ranks. Appendicitis is slowly becoming a global phenomenon.
