Preface

This section is openly critical of free will. If you're unwilling to entertain the possibility that free will is in fewer places than we tend to think, then don't read this page. If you're interested in fortifying your own humility or extending compassion toward the luckless, then please read on.

What is Cosmic Morality?

Cosmic morality is my own term for deserved praise or blame perceived as a result of one's own volition and nothing else. To make that clearer, cosmic morality is when someone thinks they deserve praise and someone else deserves blame because volition is the only factor in their actions. This often fuels a sense of superiority, even when she who is superior is more lucky in circumstance and generally more gifted in her genetic endowment. This perception of the world primes us to consider the homeless more lazy and the incarcerated morally depraved. Reinventing and reinvigorating humility demands a closer look at free will.

Free Will

Free will is a widely controversial and sensitive topic. Questioning the existence of free will is likely to be met with a lifted brow or two. German philosopher Arthur Schopenhauer remarked, “A man can do as he wills, but not will as he wills.” This is quite clear, even bold for that matter. Surely everyone freely chooses their study in university, right? Well, more options are available than the one they choose, and they could have just as easily picked one of those. I’ve italicized “easily” because the option is available, but no physics hater will pick physics. We could choose the pistachio ice cream over vanilla or chocolate, but will we so much as entertain that idea? Of course not! Imagine a woman who kills her husband by mixing arsenic into his drink; could she have done otherwise if we rewound the clock of time? It does not seem likely. The simple principle of cause and effect seems to be forgotten when discussing free will. The action potential of a neuron has consequences for other neurons to which it is linked, which have consequences for even other neurons to which those are linked. This domino effect does not yield to an uncaused neuron. This might strike us as reductive, but extraordinary evidence is required to accept free will. Charles Whitman, a man known as the “Texas Tower Sniper”, uncharacteristically stabbed the hearts of his wife and mother. He then shot several others at a Texas university before scaling the clocktower to mow down over thirty people; this took place for approximately an hour and a half before Whitman was shot by police. Sometime after he was possessed by this rabid behavior, he wrote a note that authorities should take a particular interest in his brain during autopsy, speculating that something was neurologically dysfunctional:

“I do not quite understand what it is that compels me to type this letter. Perhaps it is to leave some vague reason for the actions I have recently performed. I do not really understand myself these days. I am supposed to be an average reasonable and intelligent young man. However, lately (I cannot recall when it started) I have been a victim of many unusual and irrational thoughts. These thoughts constantly recur, and it requires a tremendous mental effort to concentrate on useful and progressive tasks.”

The autopsy request was approved in 1966. Urine and blood samples were tested for drugs and other physiological abnormalities that might suggest a cause for erratic behavior. Nothing abnormal was found except a “pecan-sized” tumor pressing against Whitman’s brain. Experts could not definitively decide whether the tumor had been the culprit, but it was likely. Forensic specialists, apparently having no way to inquire the exact location of the tumor, theorized that it pressed against the amygdala, the fear, aggression, and anxiety center of the brain. These happen to be the emotions to which some of our most valuable adaptations can be attributed: vigilance, carefulness, motivation, and retaliation to deter further harm coming our way; without them, individuals become a husk of their former selves (as in the case of amygdalectomy victims), or impulsively engage an opponent physically at risk to themselves (as in the case of low serotonin individuals). Whitman made victims of many people, but he too was utterly luckless.

Evidence supports that Whitman is not alone in being a victim of his own brain. According to the CDC, 25 - 87% of inmates have sustained a traumatic brain injury (TBI) compared to 8.5% in the general population. As a result, inmates experience high rates of anxiety, depression, and impulsive aggression, as well as altered speech and difficulty understanding correctional officers and other prisoners. TBI is also more common among the homeless, as well as a significantly low IQ. It should also be noted that a low serotonin level also predicts lawless behavior and imprisonment.

The space in which free will resides has become narrower over time. Disorders such as schizophrenia and autism were once explained as having been caused by “refrigerator mothers.” That is, cold-hearted mothers who did not provide their child with enough love or social interaction. Psychologists explained this to the child’s parents, passing on the consensus of the scientific literature. Upon the discovery of schizophrenia as a genetic disorder, these psychologists apologized to the many parents whose years had been wracked with torment over the supposed mistreatment of their children. Epilepsy was well understood by doctors several hundred years ago; it was, of course, a result of the epileptic’s consorting with Beelzebub. Epilepsy is better understood as a genetic disorder, once again. Rather than burning epileptics at the stake, epileptics are made not to drive for some time if they’ve recently seized while driving. If there's reason to believe that an epileptic may hurt herself and others while driving, she's temporarily kept off of the road for safety purposes with no devil-related accusations thrown her way. Individuals with psychiatric disorders were often put on trial as witches. One clever witch detection method had the crucifixion story read to supposed witches; not to find the story tear-jerking was a death sentence. One physician sent out a message acknowledging that this holocaust of witches was clearly necessary, but there was one problem with the crucifixion story method: nasolacrimal ducts in many older women become less functional, preventing the flow of tears. In other words, non-witches were being burnt at the stake due to physiological dysfunction. Over time, we've gradually grasped the ways in which our capacity as the ultimate arbiters of our own behavior is undermined. The list of restrictions on free will continues to grow over time; it will not be long before our tally of free choices is infinitesimal.

“I returned and saw under the sun, that the race is not to the swift, nor the battle to the strong, neither yet bread to the wise, nor yet riches to men of understanding, nor yet favour to men of skill; but time and chance happeneth to them all.” – Ecclesiastes 9:11

Everyone is comfortable relieving individuals of responsibility when disorders undermine their volition. This is progress. It's true that genetic determinism is nonsense, but the introduction of behavioral variation in the forms of epigenetics and neuroplasticity, among others, does not provide any freedom of will. Let's assume that abuse is required for a behavioral change owing to neuroanatomy (such as an enlarged amygdala and the paranoia, aggressiveness, and seemingly irrational emotions it brings along for the ride); yes, the abuse or absence of abuse by parents has its consequences, but the parents’ cosmic responsibility in both cases is nil. The child’s circumstances of life are bettered or worsened by the parents’ actions, but to speak of the parents’ responsibility is futile. The parents’ parents, friends, and other social partners also had influences on their development, including epigenetic changes that take place in the womb. Diet, too, is responsible for epigenetic changes. However, responsibility still does not lie in the hands of the grocery shopper. Her inclinations, mood, and predilections to the bakery's many confections, among other factors beyond conscious control, are shackles which inhibit her free choice.

It is those who take great pride in their accomplishments, whether material or moral, who cling to free will to assert superiority of will over their fellow human beings. This is a shame.
